Background
Operations First. Architecture Second.

Before AI systems architecture, there were 25 years of operational leadership. Managing $14M media budgets at Royal Caribbean International. Driving a 26% acquisition lift at Cooper DDB for Carnival Corporation. Building operational systems at Norwegian Cruise Lines. Running founder-led businesses where the decisions, the strategy, and the consequences all landed on one desk.

That background shapes every architecture decision. Systems that look elegant on a whiteboard but break under operational pressure are worthless. Systems that work when the founder is unavailable, when the team is under stress, when the edge case nobody planned for shows up at 4pm on a Friday — those are worth building.

Three years ago, the focus shifted to AI systems architecture — specifically, the question of what must exist before AI can safely execute inside a business workflow. The answer was always the same: authority must be explicit, verification must be independent, and the system must work even when the person who designed it isn't watching.
